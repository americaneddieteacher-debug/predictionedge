The Prediction-Market Calibration Starter Kit

The one skill that separates disciplined forecasters from gamblers — and the exact journal and prompts we use to stay honest.

Walk-forward audited · paper-traded · tracked publicly

Most people lose on prediction markets for one boring reason: they bet on what they want to happen, not on where their honest probability differs from the price.

This kit teaches calibration and hands you the journal and prompts we use. It will not make you rich this week. It is designed to make you measurable — so if you do have an edge, you can prove it to yourself instead of guessing.

This is education and research, not financial advice. Nothing here is a recommendation to buy or sell any contract. Markets can and do go against well-reasoned positions. Only risk money you can afford to lose, and check your local eligibility (Kalshi is a regulated US exchange; availability varies by state and country).

1The one equation that runs everything

A contract that costs 60¢ for YES is the market saying: “we think this is 60% likely.” That price is a probability. So your job is never “will this happen?” — it’s “is the crowd’s probability wrong, and which way?”

Market implied probability   P_mkt = contract price (¢) / 100   # 60¢ YES → 60%
Your calibrated probability   P_me  = your honest estimate (Part 2)
Your edge                     Edge  = P_me − P_mkt               # act only when this clears costs

YES payoff:  profit = (100 − price),  loss = price               # bet 60¢ → win 40¢ or lose 60¢

Three rules fall out of this, and they are the whole game:

  1. If P_me ≈ P_mkt, there is no edge — do nothing. Most contracts, most days, are no-bets. That is correct.
  2. An edge only counts if it survives costs. A 2-point edge that costs 3 points to capture is a losing bet wearing a winner’s costume.
  3. The best bet is often the NO-bet you don’t hype. When the crowd prices a YES at 0.85+ on a hyper-specific outcome (“unemployment will be exactly 5.6%”), it is usually overconfident about precision. Fading that — buying the cheap NO — is the core of our public track record.
Analogy: the market price is a weather forecast thousands already argued over. You don’t get paid for agreeing it’ll probably rain. You get paid only when you can show, over many days, that when the forecast said 85% it actually rained 70% of the time — and you bet the difference.

2Calibration: the skill, and how to measure it

Calibration = when you say 70%, it happens 70% of the time. An uncalibrated forecaster who says “90% sure!” and is right 60% of the time goes broke even when they feel confident. You measure it with one number you can compute in a spreadsheet: the Brier score.

Brier score = average of (P_me − outcome)²     outcome = 1 if YES, 0 if NO

Lower is better.  0.00 = perfect.  0.25 = no better than guessing 50/50.

Worked example — five resolved bets:

Your P_meOutcome (1=YES)(P_me − outcome)²
0.8010.04
0.6000.36
0.9010.01
0.3000.09
0.5510.2025

Brier = (0.04 + 0.36 + 0.01 + 0.09 + 0.2025) / 5 = 0.1405. That 0.36 row is the lesson: you said 60% YES and it resolved NO. One overconfident miss dominates the score. The fastest way to lower your Brier score is not to be more right — it’s to stop being overconfident.

The overconfidence check (do this every month)

Pull every bet where you said 90%+. Count how many actually resolved that way. Almost everyone discovers their “90% sure” bucket only lands ~70–75% of the time. That gap is leaking money. Rule: until your 90% bucket actually hits ~90%, treat your own high-confidence YES calls as suspect and size them down — or pass.

3The Calibration Journal (your most valuable asset)

Track every position — paper or real — in one table. Thirty rows is the minimum before any conclusion means anything; below that you are reading noise.

Fill P_me and the pre-note before you stare at the price. Anchoring to the market first quietly turns “my estimate” into “the price, plus a nudge” — and then you’re measuring nothing.

The walk-forward habit (how pros avoid fooling themselves)

At ~30 rows, split the journal in two by date. Find your patterns (“sharp on jobless claims, terrible on politics”) in the older half only. Then check whether the pattern still holds in the newer half. If a pattern only looks good on the data you used to find it, it’s a hindsight illusion, not an edge. Find on old, prove on new — always.

4Three diagnostic prompts (paste into Claude or any LLM)

These turn a chat model into a disciplined check on your own reasoning. (The full pack — sizing/Kelly math, the idea-to-rule converter, and the loss-autopsy mentor — is in the premium tier.)

Prompt 1 — Calibration & overconfidence audit

Role: You audit a prediction-market forecaster's calibration.
Measure the HONESTY of their probabilities, not their win rate.

INPUT
• Log: date | market | my P_me | market P_mkt | entry price | result (1/0)
• Sample size: [N]   (if under 30, say plainly it's statistically too early)

OUTPUT
1. Brier score = mean((P_me − result)^2). Closer to 0 is better.
2. Calibration curve: bucket P_me into 10% bands; compare predicted vs actual hit rate.
3. Overconfidence check: actual hit rate of my 90%+ bucket.
4. My Brier vs the market's Brier (using P_mkt). I only have an edge if mine is lower.
5. One concrete weakness, stated bluntly.

⚠ If you change a rule from this, validate it on a NEW period — not this data.

Prompt 2 — Adversarial thesis red-team (run before every bet)

Role: You are an adversarial reviewer trying to BREAK my bet. No praise.

MY THESIS
• Market: [exact question + resolution wording]
• Position: [YES/NO] @ [price], P_me = [%]   • Reasoning: [why I see an edge]

ATTACK
1. Base rate: how often does this type of event actually happen?
2. What the market knows: at [X]¢, what does the crowd see that I might miss?
3. Three concrete paths where this resolves AGAINST me.
4. Resolution trap: did I re-read the EXACT settlement criteria?
5. Story vs data: narrative or evidence? Name the bias.
6. Direct verdict: is there a REAL edge here, yes or no, and why.

Prompt 3 — Hunch → falsifiable, bettable rule

Role: Convert a vague market hunch into a precise, falsifiable position.

MY HUNCH: [e.g. "inflation is cooling faster than people expect"]

CONVERT
1. The specific market(s) that express this (exact question + resolution).
2. Restate as falsifiable: "If [measurable condition], then YES." Strip vague words.
3. Free signal sources that update this (FRED, EIA, official releases, polls).
4. Update rule: which signal moves my P_me, and by how many points — defined in advance.
5. Entry threshold: minimum edge vs price before I act (e.g. ≥ 8 points).
6. The conditions where this thesis FAILS — and the filter that keeps me out.
The LLM is a sparring partner, not an oracle. It can reason about your logic and base rates, but it does not know tomorrow’s CPI print. Treat every output as a challenge to survive, not an answer to obey.

5Your first week

  1. Day 1: Open a spreadsheet with the Part 3 columns. Pick one category you actually follow.
  2. Days 1–7: Log 1–2 paper positions a day. Write the pre-note first. Run the Prompt 2 red-team before committing each.
  3. End of week 1: You won’t have 30 rows yet — fine. Just confirm the habit: did you set P_me before anchoring to the price?
  4. At ~30 settled rows: run Prompt 1, compute Brier, do the overconfidence check, split-test your patterns. Now you have a real signal about whether you have an edge.
